# Chapter 1

The dust coated Cornelius Drake’s scales with a film of ochre grime that he found personally offensive. He marched along the winding dirt track with precise, indignant steps, his wings folded tight against his flanks to prevent the brush of olive branches from smudging his burnished bronze hide further. The path wound through the ancient grove like a serpent, and with every twist, the humidity climbed, pressing against his snout with the subtlety of a damp wool blanket.

"I must register a formal complaint," Cornelius announced, adjusting his custom spectacles with a claw. The lenses, ground specifically to correct his vision for the delicate work of cataloging, slid down his snout despite his efforts. He pushed them up again. "This atmosphere is unacceptable. The moisture content must be approaching eighty percent. Do you understand what this will do to the parchment? The glue in the bindings? The very integrity of the collection I have agreed to bring?"

Zephyr "Zeph" Taraki trudged three paces ahead, his shoulders hunched under a canvas pack that clanked with the sound of metal cookware. The boy did not turn around. "Yeah, Boss. You mentioned it. Like, twelve times since the library."

"And I shall mention it a thirteenth if necessary," Cornelius said. He stepped over a root that had erupted through the soil like a gnarled knuckle. "The travel chests are sealed with wax, but even wax has its limits. I have first editions in there. First editions, Zeph. They are not designed for jungle conditions."

"We're in an olive grove," Zeph mumbled. He kicked a stone, sending it skittering into the underbrush. "And it's not even that far from town. Just because you can't see the water doesn't mean the air changes."

"The air changes precisely when one cannot see the water," Cornelius corrected. He flexed his toes, feeling the grit between his claws. "The absence of the thermal regulation provided by the coastal breeze creates a pocket of stagnation. It is elementary physics. One would think a student of your standing would comprehend basic meteorological principles."

Zeph stopped walking. He turned, his dark face flushed from exertion, sweat beading on his forehead. The boy's unruly black hair, which possessed an unnatural metallic bronze sheen that Cornelius suspected was the result of some cheap cosmetic product, stuck to his temples. "Look, we're almost there, okay? Can we just... not? For like, five minutes? The kids are going to be way worse than humidity."

Cornelius preened a scale on his forearm, smoothing it back into alignment. "I am attempting to establish parameters. Order must be imposed before the chaos descends. Once we reach this rustic encampment, there will be no time for proper organization. I must ensure you understand the severity of our archival situation."

"Sure," Zeph said. He turned and resumed walking. "Super severe."

The path opened without warning. One moment they were enclosed in a tunnel of silver-green leaves, the next they stood in a clearing that Cornelius assessed with a critical eye. The Old Olive Press sprawled before them, a collection of structures that offended his sense of architectural symmetry. The Stone Villa, which would serve as the Lodge, leaned slightly to the left, its pink plaster crumbling in a manner that suggested terminal decline rather than rustic charm. The Treehouse Cabins perched in the branches of the ancient olives like abandoned nests, connected by rope bridges that swayed in a breeze Cornelius could not feel but could see moving the leaves. The Pergola, an open-air stone patio dominated by a massive circular press stone, teemed with activity.

Chaos had already arrived.

"Health code violation," said a voice to Cornelius's left. "The stone slab shows evidence of insufficient sterilization. I can see organic matter in the crevices. Likely olive residue from previous seasons. Unsanitary."

Cornelius turned his head. Little Kyria Varka stood with a notebook pressed against her chest, her tusks—small and polished to a pearly sheen for one so young—gleaming as she wrote with a pencil that looked like a matchstick in her orcish grip. She wore a crisp white tunic that appeared freshly pressed despite the hike, and her hair was braided with military precision.

"Kyria," Cornelius said. "I was not informed you would be conducting inspections prior to the official start of the camp."

"I go where the violations are," Kyria said. She snapped her notebook shut. "The Council sent me to observe. My aunt wants to know if this 'experimental culinary program' is worth the grant money. So far, I see dust, insects, and what appears to be moss growing on the north-facing wall of that barn."

"That is patina," Cornelius said. "It adds character."

"It adds spores," Kyria corrected. She walked toward the Villa, her boots crunching on the gravel.

Zeph exhaled through his nose. "Great. We have a spy."

"We have an observer," Cornelius amended. "And we shall give her nothing to observe but perfect efficiency. Where is the inventory manifest?"

"I have it," Imelda Kassiani called from the Villa's porch.

The Head Librarian stood in the shade of the overhang, her silver-white hair catching the light in intricate braids adorned with copper beads. At one hundred eighty-seven years, Imelda possessed the kind of stillness that made the air around her seem calmer. Her nut-brown skin, lined with the maps of centuries, creased as she smiled. She held a clipboard in one hand and a cup of mint tea in the other.

"Everything is accounted for," Imelda said. "The books are in the library tent. The kitchen supplies are under lock and key. The sleeping arrangements are... adequate."

"Adequate is not a word I associate with success," Cornelius said. He trotted up the steps, his claws clicking against the terracotta tiles. "Show me the library tent. I must ensure the humidity has not already begun its destructive work."

"After the opening circle," Imelda said. "The children are gathering."

Cornelius's tail twitched. "Already? We have not established protocols. We have not distributed the schedules. We have not—"

"Relax, Lizard," said a new voice. "You're going to pop a scale."

Cornelius froze. He turned slowly, his golden eyes narrowing behind his spectacles. A young satyr-kin leaned against the Villa's railing, his hooves crossed at the ankles, his fingers drumming a rhythm against the wood that Cornelius found instantly irritating. The boy's hair was dyed a shade of blue that occurred nowhere in nature, and his grin displayed too many teeth.

"Zeno," Cornelius said. "I presume. You are early."

"Got here last night," Zeno said. He pushed off the railing and landed on the tiles with a clatter of hooves. "Wanted to scope out the kitchen. You know, for strategic purposes."

"Strategic purposes do not involve premature access to the facilities," Cornelius said. "And please stop tapping. That rhythm is irregular."

Zeno increased the tempo. "This? This is just energy, Boss. I've got lots of it."

"Contain it," Cornelius commanded. "Where are the others? We have a schedule."

"They're around," Zeno said. He pointed toward the Pergola. "Lukas is already trying to raid the snack stores. Thais is measuring the angles of the roof with some kind of instrument. And the quiet one... Pippa, right? She's under a tree reading something with sparkles on it."

"Reading is acceptable," Cornelius said. "Raiding stores is not. Measuring angles is..." He paused. "Acceptable, provided she does not damage the structure. We shall begin the orientation immediately."

Cornelius marched toward the central gathering space, a circular area of packed earth surrounded by low stone walls that served as benches. The campers were indeed assembling, though "assembling" suggested a level of organization that did not exist. A tall, thin girl with her hair pulled back in a severe bun stood with her back to the group, sketching the Villa's facade onto a pad with aggressive strokes of charcoal. Thais Doukas, Cornelius remembered from the roster. Artistic temperament. Potentially disruptive.

A boy of perhaps fourteen sat on one of the stone walls, his legs swinging, a crumb of something stuck to the corner of his mouth. Lukas Miller. He waved at Cornelius with a sticky hand.

"Hey, little dragon!" Lukas called. "You brought the books?"

"I am not 'little,'" Cornelius said. "I am precisely the correct size for my species. And yes, the books are secured. Do not touch them."

"Sure, sure," Lukas said. He wiped his mouth with his sleeve, leaving a streak of sugar. "Got any snacks in there? I'm starving."

"The nutritional schedule will be distributed at the appropriate time," Cornelius said. He climbed onto the central stone press, which served as a podium, and spread his wings to their full three-foot span. The gesture was meant to command attention. It mostly attracted flies.

Zeph hovered at the edge of the circle, looking miserable. Imelda stood by the Villa's entrance, watching with her patient expression. Little Kyria had positioned herself on a wall with her notebook ready.

"Welcome to Camp Olea," Cornelius began. His voice carried the authority of one accustomed to shushing noisy patrons in marble halls. "I am Cornelius Drake, Archivist and Administrator of this program. For the next two weeks, this facility will operate under strict guidelines designed to maximize educational output while minimizing risk to life, limb, and literature. You will find your schedules posted in the common area. You will adhere to the quiet hours. You will not—absolutely not—tamper with the magical ingredients in the kitchen without direct supervision."

Zeno raised his hand. "What if it's an accident?"

"There are no accidents," Cornelius said. "There are only failures of preparation. You will address me as Master Drake, or Sir. You will address Counselor Zeph as..."

He paused. He had not actually decided what Zeph should be called.

"Zeph is fine," Zeph muttered.

"Counselor Taraki," Cornelius overrode. "We shall begin with the inventory of supplies. Form a single file line. No talking. No fidgeting. We shall proceed with dignity."

The children stared at him. Thais continued sketching. Lukas picked his nose. Zeno vibrated in place, his hooves scraping against the stone.

"Now," Cornelius said.

Nobody moved.

"I said," Cornelius repeated, his voice dropping to a dangerous register, "form a line."

Zeno broke first. The satyr-kin snorted, a sound like a bellows wheezing, and reached into the pouch at his belt. He withdrew a handful of something white and doughy.

"Catch!" Zeno shouted.

The dough struck Cornelius square in the chest.

Silence fell over the circle. The dough, a sticky, yeasty mass intended for the afternoon's baking lesson, clung to Cornelius's scales. It was warm. It smelled of fermentation and rebellion.

Cornelius looked down at the affront. He looked up at Zeno. The boy was grinning, waiting for a reaction, his eyes bright with the particular madness of adolescence.

"That," Cornelius said slowly, "was a mistake."

He inhaled. His draconic heritage provided certain biological advantages, among them a breath weapon that, while not of the magnitude possessed by his distant cousins in the Capital City—those bus-sized monsters who could melt stone—was sufficient to cauterize wounds, solder metal, and intimidate unruly patrons who refused to pay overdue fines.

Cornelius opened his jaws and exhaled.

A tiny blue flame, no larger than a candle's flicker, wavered in the air before his snout. It danced for a moment, made a sound like a dying sigh, and vanished.

The silence that followed was profound.

Then Zeno laughed. It was not a mean laugh, precisely, but it was loud, and it was joined by Lukas's guffaw and even a snort from Thais, who had finally turned around.

"Nice trick, Lizard!" Zeno crowed. "Do it again!"

Cornelius stood frozen on the stone. His chest felt tight, and not from the effort of breathing fire. The humidity, he told himself. It was the humidity affecting his internal chemistry. Dragons required dry air to properly ignite their breath. This was a known fact. This was not a reflection of his stature, his age, or his increasingly diminished capacity to project authority.

He stepped down from the stone. The dough fell from his chest with a wet plop.

"I shall be in the library tent," Cornelius said. His voice was steady. Precise. "Zeph, you will supervise the... the whatever this is. Imelda, a word."

He did not run. He walked with dignity toward the canvas structure that had been erected behind the Villa, designated as the temporary library. His tail dragged slightly, stirring the dust. He did not look back.

The library tent was a disaster waiting to happen. Cornelius pushed through the flap and surveyed the interior with a despair that bordered on the existential. The shelves were wooden planks balanced on crates. The books sat in sealed wax-canvas bags, sweating in the heat. The floor was dirt.

"Unacceptable," Cornelius whispered.

He moved methodically, because method was all he had. He checked the seals on the bags. He arranged the inventory sheets in alphabetical order. He placed his spectacles case on the small camp table at exactly ninety degrees to the edge. He sat on his haunches and stared at the canvas wall.

From outside, he could hear Zeph attempting to rally the campers. The boy's voice cracked as he tried to organize a game. The children shouted over him. Someone screamed with delight. A heavy object—possibly Zeno—crashed into something hollow.

Cornelius closed his eyes. He was a Library Dragon. He was Draco bibliotheca. He had guarded the Solterra Bay collection for seventy-three years. He had faced book thieves, paper mites, and the Great Flood of '09. He could manage a group of children for two weeks.

He could.

He would.

He opened his eyes and adjusted his spectacles.

The noise outside subsided gradually as the afternoon wore on. Cornelius busied himself with cataloging the emergency reserve texts, his claws clicking against the paper with a rhythm that soothed his nerves. He marked the condition of each volume with meticulous care. He sorted the spare index cards by subject. He ignored the cramp in his wings and the persistent, gnawing sense that he was in over his head.

Evening arrived with the sound of ceramic bowls being distributed in the dining area. Cornelius emerged from his tent to supervise the meal, his composure rebuilt layer by layer like the scales on his hide. He inspected the stew for foreign objects. He ensured the bread was sliced uniformly. He sat at the head of the rough-hewn table and watched the campers eat with a vigilance that made several of them nervous.

Zeno, he noted, had been seated between Imelda and Zeph, effectively neutralized by soft power and strategic positioning. The boy ate with his hands, but at least he was not throwing anything.

Pippa sat at the far end of the table, small and quiet, her pigtails messy with twigs. She had a book propped against her water jug, and she read while she ate, her eyes flicking back and forth across the pages with an intensity that Cornelius found slightly more acceptable than the others' behavior. Fairy tales, he noted. The Brothers Grimm. Dark stuff for a child, but at least she was literate.

After the meal, the campers dispersed. Imelda organized a washing station. Zeph attempted to establish a fire pit protocol. Cornelius patrolled the perimeter, ensuring the boundaries were secure.

He found Lukas sitting alone on the steps of the Sanctuary Barn, looking bored. The boy kicked at the dirt with his heels.

"I require a task," Lukas announced.

"You require patience," Cornelius replied.

"I'm bored. Zeno won't let me help with the pranks because I'm 'too loud.' Thais won't let me see her drawings. And the new kid—Pippa—she just reads."

"Reading is a noble pursuit," Cornelius said. "You should try it."

"Books are boring," Lukas said. "Unless they have pictures of food."

Cornelius sighed, a sound like wind through dry leaves. "Return to the common area. I believe Counselor Taraki is organizing a stargazing activity."

"Yeah, yeah," Lukas said. He didn't move.

Cornelius left him there. He had more pressing concerns. The library tent required locking down for the night. He had brought a special clasp, dwarven-made, that could secure the flap against intrusion. He fitted it now, testing the mechanism three times to ensure it held.

"All secure?" Imelda asked from behind him.

"Obviously," Cornelius said. He did not turn. "The collection is safe. The humidity is... manageable. I have established order."

"Have you?" Imelda's voice carried that gentle, infuriating amusement that always made Cornelius feel as though she could see through his scales to the insecurity beneath. "The children seem energetic."

"The children are barbarians," Cornelius said. "But they are contained barbarians. Zeno is under watch. Kyria is documenting violations rather than causing them. The Miller boy is merely hungry, not malicious. And the girl... Pippa... she is harmless. She reads."

"She does," Imelda agreed. "She has been reading all afternoon. Under the big olive tree. I gave her a bag of those iridescent sprinkles Cassius sent up for the baking demonstration. She said she wanted to decorate some cookies for the other children."

Cornelius nodded. "A constructive use of time. Decorative arts require precision. She will learn discipline."

"Discipline," Imelda repeated. "Yes. I am sure she will."

Imelda walked away, her sandals making soft sounds on the tiles. Cornelius checked the lock one final time. He looked out over the camp. The Treehouse Cabins were darkening silhouettes against the sky. The Pergola stood empty, the stone press gleaming in the twilight. Everything was in its place. Everything was controlled.

He did not see Pippa in the shadows beneath the ancient olive tree, her book open on her knees, her hands cupped around a handful of cookies that glittered with an unnatural dust. He did not see her whisper to the crumbs, her voice soft and earnest, weaving a story about princes and transformations and the magic of making things special. He did not see her offer the cookies to Lukas, who had wandered over, complaining of hunger, his eyes lighting up at the sight of the sprinkles.

He did not see the boy eat.

Cornelius turned and entered the Stone Villa, satisfied that his system was intact, that the chaos was outside, and that the night would pass in dignified silence.

He locked the door behind him.

# Chapter 2

 Chapter 2

Cornelius Drake awoke precisely at dawn, his internal chronometer—a biological advantage of his species that he considered superior to any mechanical timepiece—rousing him from a sleep of immaculate depth. He lay on his portable sleeping mat in the corner of the Stone Villa's common room, his wings folded tight against his flanks, his tail curled in a perfect spiral. For seven blessed seconds, he existed in perfect equilibrium: the camp was secure, the books were sealed, the humidity had not yet begun its daily assault.

Then he heard the singing.

It was not good singing. It was enthusiastic singing, which was worse. Someone—multiple someones, based on the harmonic chaos—was belting out a sea shanty in the vicinity of the kitchen pergola. Cornelius's left eye twitched. He rose, shook each limb in sequence to ensure proper circulation, and adjusted his spectacles. The lenses, fogged slightly from the temperature differential between his body and the morning air, cleared after three wipes against his forearm.

He stepped onto the Villa's porch and surveyed his domain.

The camp had survived the night. This was his first observation, and he permitted himself a small puff of satisfaction—warm air, no flame, merely a gesture of contentment. The Treehouse Cabins stood intact. The Sanctuary Barn remained upright. The library tent, visible beyond the Villa's corner, still bore his dwarven lock undisturbed.

The singing, however, continued.

"Zeph," Cornelius called.

The boy emerged from behind the Villa, carrying two buckets of water. His hair, that unfortunate metallic bronze, stood in peaks that suggested he had slept in a windstorm. "Morning, Boss."

"Who is singing?"

"Zeno. And Lukas. They're helping Cassius set up for the baking lesson."

"Helping," Cornelius repeated. "Zeno. Helping."

"They're peeling apples. Sort of. Zeno's doing more throwing than peeling, but Cassius seems okay with it."

Cornelius descended the steps, his claws clicking against the terracotta. "The satyr-kin requires supervision. Constant supervision. His definition of 'help' and my definition of 'help' diverge significantly."

"Yeah, well, Cassius is in charge of the kitchen," Zeph said. "You said so yourself. 'Cassius manages the culinary curriculum. We manage the literature.' Direct quote."

Cornelius's tail twitched. The boy had a memory for statements that inconvenienced him. "I shall observe. Observation is not interference."

The Pergola occupied the center of the camp like a stone stage, its roof of woven grapevines filtering the morning light into shifting patterns. Cassius Nerva stood at the central press stone, his hooves—broader and more stable than Zeno's adolescent versions—planted in a baker's stance. The satyr-kin was forty-eight, though he appeared younger, his curly hair more grey than brown, his eyes perpetually worried. He wore an apron that had survived three decades of kitchen disasters, its fabric stiff with the accumulated residue of heroic baking.

"More cinnamon," Cassius was saying. "No, not that much. Lukas, please stop eating the filling."

"I need to test it," Lukas said. He stood at the edge of the press stone, a paring knife in one hand, a half-eaten apple in the other. His shirt bore the evidence of previous meals in a constellation of stains. "Quality control."

"You've tested six apples. We need twelve for the filling."

Zeno danced—there was no other word for his movement—around the beehive ovens, his blue hair catching the light. He held a wooden spoon like a conductor's baton. "The dough is rising! I can feel it! It's alive!"

"It is yeast," Cornelius said, entering the Pergola. "A fungus. Single-celled organisms consuming sugar and producing carbon dioxide. Not alive in any meaningful sense."

Cassius looked up, his expression brightening with the relief of reinforcements. "Cornelius! Good. You're here for the Giggle-Glaze demonstration. The Council approved it last month—low-risk magical baking, minimal side effects, educational value."

"I reviewed the permit," Cornelius said. "Levitation not to exceed three feet. Duration not to exceed ninety seconds. Laughter compulsions to cease upon ingestion of neutralizing agent." He produced a small notebook from his vest pocket—he had added pockets to the garment specifically for documentation purposes—and clicked his pen. "I shall monitor for compliance."

"Great," Cassius said. "Perfect. Zeno, step back from the oven. The glaze is cooling."

The Giggle-Glaze Donuts represented, in Cornelius's assessment, an acceptable compromise between magical education and physical safety. The recipe derived from traditional Cyprian festival baking, modified with alchemical stabilizers to produce predictable effects. Consumption caused spontaneous levitation and uncontrollable laughter—harmless, temporary, and memorable. The perfect introduction to controlled magical cuisine.

Cassius worked with the precision of decades, measuring powdered giggleroot against refined sugar, folding the mixture into a glaze that shimmered with faint iridescence. Not the iridescence of Pippa's sprinkles—Cornelius had noted those in his inventory—but the softer, more predictable glow of certified alchemical ingredients.

"Now," Cassius said, addressing the assembled campers. Thais had appeared, her sketchbook ready. Pippa sat on a low wall, her book open, her eyes flicking between the pages and the demonstration. Little Kyria occupied her usual position, notebook poised. "The Giggle-Glaze affects everyone differently based on body mass and magical sensitivity. Zeno, as a satyr-kin, will likely experience enhanced effects. Lukas, as a human with no known magical lineage, should have a baseline response."

"Give me the big one," Lukas said.

"Portion control is mandatory," Cornelius interjected. "The permit specifies—"

"One donut per student," Cassius said smoothly. "Yes. I have it here." He distributed the pastries with the evenhandedness of long practice, placing a modest-sized donut on each plate. "Zeno. Lukas. Thais. Pippa. And one for me, to demonstrate that the effect is temporary and harmless."

Zeno seized his donut and bit into it before Cassius had finished speaking. For three seconds, nothing happened. Then the satyr-kin's eyes widened. A sound emerged from his throat—not laughter, precisely, but a high-pitched squeak that escalated in frequency until it passed beyond human hearing range. His hooves left the ground. He rose, spinning slowly, his arms windmilling for balance that his body no longer required.

"This," Zeno announced, his voice returning in a squeaky register, "is the best thing that has ever happened to me!"

Lukas had finished his donut with less theatricality but equal speed. He floated upward more sedately, his expression of surprise giving way to delight. "I'm flying! I'm actually flying!" He paddled at the air as if swimming. "Look! Look at me!"

Thais rose with mathematical precision, her sketchbook clutched to her chest, her legs crossed at the ankles. She continued sketching, now working from an elevated perspective. "The light is better up here," she observed.

Pippa nibbled her donut with small, careful bites. She rose only six inches, just enough to make her pigtails swing. She giggled, a sound like wind chimes, and returned her attention to her book.

Cornelius made notes. Heights: Zeno at 2.7 feet, Lukas at 2.1 feet, Thais at 2.4 feet, Pippa at 0.5 feet. Duration: approaching sixty seconds. Behavioral effects: consistent with permit specifications. He permitted himself a second puff of satisfaction. This was controlled chaos. This was educational magic functioning as designed.

Cassius, floating at a dignified 1.8 feet, smiled at Cornelius. "See? Harmless. Fun. The children will remember this."

"The permit expires in forty minutes," Cornelius said. "Please administer the neutralizing agent."

They descended gradually, the giggleroot metabolizing, laughter fading to hiccups. Zeno landed with a clatter of hooves and immediately demanded another donut. Lukas, slower to return to full gravity, stumbled slightly, his hand reaching for the plate of remaining pastries.

"One per student," Cornelius reminded him.

"But there are extras," Lukas said. "Look, six left. They'll go stale."

"Tomorrow's demonstration," Cassius said, moving the plate to a high shelf. "Giggle-Glaze Muffins. Different texture, same effect."

Lukas's face fell with theatrical completeness. "I'm still hungry."

"You are perpetually hungry," Cornelius said. "This is a biological constant, not an emergency. Occupy yourself with literature until the midday meal."

"Books," Lukas muttered. "Right."

The morning proceeded with unusual smoothness. Cornelius supervised the transfer of several reference volumes from the sealed travel chests to the library tent, where he arranged them by subject and condition. Zeno, surprisingly, did not attempt sabotage—perhaps the Giggle-Glaze had satisfied his need for disruption. He spent an hour attempting to teach Pippa a satyr dance, which she watched with polite confusion before returning to her reading.

At lunch, Cornelius noted that Lukas consumed three portions of bean soup and continued to eye the high shelf where the leftover donuts waited. The boy's hunger was remarkable. Cornelius made a note to review his file for metabolic disorders.

The afternoon brought a lecture on the history of Cyprian olive cultivation, delivered by Imelda with the calm authority that made even agricultural statistics compelling. The campers sat in the Pergola's shade, Zeno fidgeting, Thais sketching the ancient press stone, Pippa reading—always reading—some volume of dark fairy tales that Cornelius made a mental note to catalog later.

He did not see Pippa slip away during the lecture's final minutes. He did not see her approach the kitchen shelf where Cassius had stored the donuts. He was reviewing his notes on humidity control when she passed him, a small plate in her hands, her expression of helpful innocence so complete that his observation filtered it as "assisting with cleanup" rather than "acquiring restricted materials."

"Thank you, Pippa," he heard Cassius say. "Yes, those need covering. The glaze gets sticky."

She covered them. She also, though Cornelius would not learn this until much later, added something from her pocket. Something iridescent. Something that caught the light like the Giggle-Glaze but was not the Giggle-Glaze.

Evening arrived with the ritual of dinner, perimeter checks, and Cornelius's increasingly confident assessment that the camp was, finally, under control. He locked the library tent with satisfaction. He verified the Villa's security. He observed the campers retiring to their respective cabins—Zeno and Lukas to the Treehouse designated for boys, Thais and Pippa to the adjacent structure for girls.

At 10:47 PM, by his internal chronometer, Cornelius completed his final patrol and entered the Villa. He slept the sleep of the vindicated.

At 2:13 AM, the screaming began.

It was not the screaming of play. Cornelius knew the acoustic signatures of children's nightmares, of pranks gone wrong, of genuine terror. This was the third category. He was upright and moving before conscious thought intervened, his wings half-extended for balance, his spectacles askew.

The sound originated from the boys' Treehouse Cabin.

Cornelius scaled the rope bridge with undignified haste, his claws finding purchase on the woven fibers, his tail rigid for stability. The bridge swayed. He ignored it. Behind him, he heard Zeph stumbling from the Villa, Imelda's calm voice rising in inquiry, Cassius's hooves clattering on stone.

He reached the cabin door. It was latched from the inside. He could hear movement within—flapping, frantic, accompanied by Zeno's voice pitched high with panic.

"Open this door immediately!" Cornelius commanded.

"Lukas is gone!" Zeno shouted. "He's just—he's gone! And there's a bird! A crazy bird!"

"Cornelius," Imelda called from below. "The window!"

He moved to the cabin's side, where a shutter stood open to the night air. With a leap that strained his wings, he caught the sill and hauled himself through, landing on the rough wooden floor in a crouch.

The scene resolved in fragments. Zeno, pressed against the far wall, his hooves skidding for purchase. A bed, Lukas's bed, its covers thrown back. And on the pillow, where a boy's head should have rested, a pigeon.

Not a ordinary pigeon. A large pigeon, grey and iridescent in the moonlight, with eyes that held an expression Cornelius could only describe as outraged confusion. It flapped at him, rising, and he batted it aside with automatic reflex. It circled, cooing frantically, and landed on the bedpost.

"Lukas?" Cornelius demanded of Zeno. "Where is Lukas?"

"I don't know! I woke up and he was—there was just this bird! And feathers! Look!" Zeno pointed to the pillow. Small grey feathers dotted the fabric. "It was sleeping where his head was!"

Cornelius approached the bed with the methodical precision of a forensic examination. The covers were disturbed in a pattern consistent with sudden, violent movement. The pillow bore the impression of a head—he could see it, the hollow where Lukas's skull had rested. And scattered across the sheets, crumbs.

He collected one on his claw. It was donut. Giggle-Glaze donut, identifiable by the crystalline structure of the dried glaze. But there was something else. A residue, faintly shimmering, that caught the moonlight wrong.

Cornelius lowered his snout and inhaled.

The smell was wrong. Giggle-Glaze should carry notes of vanilla, sugar, and the faint peppery undertone of giggleroot. This carried salt—sea salt, sharp and mineral—and beneath it, something older. Paper. Not new paper, but ancient, the scent of vellum stored in dry caves, of scrolls untouched for centuries.

"Sea salt," Cornelius said aloud. "And old paper."

"Cornelius?" Imelda's voice from the window. "What do you smell?"

He turned to her, his golden eyes wide behind his spectacles. "Not ozone. Not giggleroot. This is—" He struggled for classification. "This is narrative magic. Story magic. Someone has introduced a mimic spell. A transformation trigger."

The pigeon on the bedpost cooed at him. It was a demanding coo, an insistent coo, the coo of someone who expected answers and was receiving none.

Cornelius looked at the bird. He looked at the empty bed. He looked at the crumbs, and the feathers, and the precise indentation on the pillow where a boy's head had rested.

"No," he whispered.

He reached out, slowly, and the pigeon allowed him to cup it in his claws. It was warm. It was heavy. It had Lukas's eyes—he was certain of it, the particular shade of brown, the slight asymmetry of the left pupil.

"Lukas," Cornelius said to the pigeon.

The bird cooed, a sound that might have been affirmation. Might have been accusation.

He reviewed his mental index of permissible statements. *The campers are present.* True. *The curriculum proceeds.* Arguably true, if one interpreted 'proceeds' broadly. *Zeno is committed to his role.* Technically accurate, if the role was 'donkey.'

*Achoo!*

Cornelius Drake arranged his third ledger of the week upon the Stone Villa’s porch table with the precision of a jeweler setting a flawed diamond. The terracotta tiles beneath his claws retained the afternoon’s warmth, but Cornelius ignored the heat. He focused on the alignment: the ledger’s spine exactly parallel to the table’s edge, the quill placed at a ninety-degree angle to the inkwell, the evidence bags arranged in ascending order by weight.

The evidence bags contained sprinkles.

He had not slept. Cornelius could see the evidence of insomnia in the boy’s posture, in the way his fingers drummed against the stone balustrade. “The one where your tail hits the floor three times fast. You’re gearing up to blame someone.”

“I am gearing up to solve a mystery,” Cornelius corrected. He capped the inkwell. “Specifically, the mystery of the narrative contamination that has transformed three campers into fauna and threatens to convert a fourth should we not intervene within forty-eight hours.”

Zeph turned. His eyes were bloodshot. “You’re going to talk to Pippa.”

It was not a question.

“I intend to interview all witnesses,” Cornelius said. He straightened his waistcoat. The bronze scales along his spine prickled, lifting slightly from his skin. “Pippa is a witness. She has been present at multiple contamination sites. She arranged the Mood-Muffins. She dusted the Floaty-Loaf. She served the Sleepy-Time Squares. The probability of coincidence approaches zero.”

“She’s nine,” Zeph said. His voice cracked. “She’s a kid. She’s Lukas’s sister. She wouldn’t—”

“She would not intend harm,” Cornelius agreed. “Intent and capability are distinct variables. One may cause catastrophe through ignorance as readily as through malice.”

Zeph stepped forward. His hands were clenched into fists. “You’re wrong. I know you’re wrong. She’s just… she’s sweet. She likes glitter. That doesn’t make her a witch.”

“I did not suggest witchcraft,” Cornelius said. “I suggested narrative contamination via decorative vector. There is a difference. One requires malice. The other requires only proximity and misunderstanding.”

The porch door opened. Imelda emerged, her silver braids clicking with copper beads. She held Pippa’s hand. The child walked beside her, small and neat, her pigtails tied with ribbons that matched the violet-green of the sprinkles. She clutched a book to her chest. The book was old, its cover soft with wear, its pages edged with gold.

“Pippa has agreed to speak with us,” Imelda said. Her voice was calm, a stone in still water. “She understands we are trying to help Zeno.”

Imelda stepped forward. She placed a hand upon Pippa’s shoulder. “The magic is responding to her belief,” Imelda said quietly. “To her need for the world to be safe and storied. It is not an attack. It is a protection spell gone feral.”

“I require a solution,” Cornelius snapped. “And solutions require causes. If Pippa did not create the dust—if she merely found it or was given it—then the creator is still at large. And that creator is the true threat.”

He gathered his ledger. He gathered his evidence bags. He looked at Pippa, who sat very still, holding her book and her vial.

“You may go,” Cornelius said. “Do not sprinkle anything else. Do not read over any food. Do you understand?”

Pippa nodded. She slid off the chair. She clutched the book to her chest like a shield.

“But the story isn’t over,” Pippa said. “The Baker’s Dozen Swans. They have to turn back. They have to. Or the baker loses everything.”

“Go,” Cornelius said, more gently than he intended.

Imelda guided Pippa toward the door. Zeph hesitated, looking back at Cornelius with an expression that hovered between betrayal and pity. Then he followed them inside.

Cornelius stood alone on the porch. He opened his ledger. He crossed out *Pippa Miller* with a single decisive line. He wrote: *External Sabotage Theory. Suspect: Little Kyria Varka. Motive: Council infiltration. Method: Supply of contaminated decorative materials.*

It made sense. It fit the pattern. It was logical.

He looked at the empty chair where Pippa had sat. He looked at the tiles, where a few grains of violet-green dust had fallen from her pocket. They glittered in the afternoon light.

Cornelius turned away. He had forty-eight hours. He had a suspect to interrogate. He marched off the porch toward the olive grove, where Little Kyria was said to be conducting her inspection.

Behind him, forgotten on the steps, Pippa’s book lay open. The pages fluttered in the breeze. A picture showed twelve swans wearing aprons, and one thirteenth swan rising from a pie crust. The text beneath was written in a looping, ancient hand.

Pippa sat on the steps. She picked up the book. She began to read aloud, her voice soft and steady, telling the story of the baker who loved his swans too much, and the spell that bound them in feathers until the right words could set them free.

She read the words exactly as they were written. The dust in her pocket swirled. The air smelled suddenly of burnt sugar and old paper.

But Cornelius did not hear. He was already gone, hunting for a villain who did not exist, while the real magic sat on the steps, reading the recipe for disaster aloud to the empty air.

# Chapter 13

Chapter 13

The carriage wheels had not yet ceased their rotation upon the gravel when Cornelius Drake identified the crest upon the door. A phoenix rendered in glazed ceramic tile—Doukas family colors, copper and azure. The artistic guild’s mark. Cornelius felt his scales prickle upright along his spine, each one snapping into rigid attention like the pages of a book slammed shut.

They were forty-eight hours early.

The carriage door swung outward. A man descended first, tall and thin, his beard braided with small clay beads that clicked together like Imelda’s copper hair ornaments. He wore a tunic of undyed linen splattered with dried clay and paint. A woman followed, carrying a portfolio case the size of a shield, her silver-streaked hair bound in a kerchief that matched the phoenix tiles.

“Mr. Doukas,” Cornelius said, stepping forward. His voice emerged steady, formal, devoid of the panic currently liquefying his internal organs. “Mrs. Doukas. Welcome to Camp Olea. You are... precipitous.”

“Ah!” Mr. Doukas extended a hand covered in kiln dust. “The dragon fellow. Thais wrote about you. Splendid. We finished the installation in Solterra early. Thought we’d surprise our girl. Is she in the amphitheater? We’ve brought her new sketching charcoals.”

Cornelius’s tail twitched three times against the gravel. “The campers are currently engaged in... immersive fieldwork. Method acting. Embodying the natural world. They are not immediately visible.”

Mrs. Doukas tilted her head. Her eyes, sharp and dark, scanned the olive grove. “How avant-garde. Nature’s Masquerade? We did a series on that in the spring. Very visceral. Very... transformative.”

“Precisely,” Cornelius said. He stepped sideways, attempting to block their view of the Sanctuary Barn. “If you will accompany me to the Refreshment Tent, Imelda will serve spoon sweets. The campers will conclude their exercises shortly.”

A flutter of wings drew his attention. Lukas—the pigeon Lukas—descended from the pergola roof. He wore a scrap of blue cloth tied around his neck like a bandana. The bird landed upon the gravel path exactly between Cornelius and the carriage, pecking at a crumb of Floaty-Loaf that Zeph must have dropped earlier.

Mr. Doukas squinted. “Is that pigeon wearing a neckerchief?”

“Avian behavioral study,” Cornelius said quickly. He advanced on Lukas, making shooing motions with his claws. “Part of the immersive curriculum. The children are studying... ornithological mimicry. Costume design. Move along, bird.”

Lukas cooed. He hopped toward Mrs. Doukas’s shoe.

Cornelius lunged. He caught the pigeon in his claws—gently, but with the firmness of a librarian retrieving a rare manuscript from a unruly patron. Lukas squirmed. The bandana slipped. Cornelius tucked the bird against his chest, shielding the telltale scrap of fabric from view. “Specimen for the biological survey,” he announced. “We shall return him to the... research aviary.”

Mrs. Doukas smiled. “How committed the children must be. Method acting as birds. Thais always did love wings. Though she preferred swans. Very architectural, swans.”

“Indeed,” Cornelius said. He could feel Lukas’s heart beating against his scales, rapid and terrified. “If you will follow me. The Refreshment Tent offers shade. And tea. Very strong tea.”

He turned, hoping to guide them away from the barn, away from the library tent where Maya the turtle resided in her ventilated box, away from Zeph’s room where Zeno the donkey was currently eating hay from a feedbag. He had taken three steps when the bray erupted from the Sanctuary Barn.

It was not a subtle sound. It was a full-throated, nasal, operatic hee-haw that rolled across the olive grove like a sonic boom.

Mr. Doukas paused. “Goodness. Is that a donkey?”

“Local fauna,” Cornelius said. His tail lashed against his own ankles. “The region supports a... robust population of wild equines. Very traditional. Very Cyprian.”

“It sounded close,” Mrs. Doukas said. She turned toward the barn. “Is it in the camp?”

“The Sanctuary Barn serves as a rehabilitation center for distressed wildlife,” Cornelius improvised. He stepped into her path, wings half-furled to block the view. “We teach the children compassionate stewardship. That particular donkey is... Method acting. As a donkey. For authenticity.”

Mr. Doukas stroked his beard. His clay beads clicked. “Deep immersion. Risky, pedagogically. One might lose oneself in the role.”

“That is why we maintain rigorous supervision,” Cornelius said. He gestured urgently toward the pergola. “Shall we? The spoon sweets await. Imelda prepares them with rose water. Very traditional. Very safe.”

He herded them forward, positioning his body between the parents and any line of sight to the barn. Imelda appeared at the pergola entrance, her silver braids gleaming, her expression serenely untroubled by the catastrophe unfolding.

“Mr. and Mrs. Doukas,” Imelda said. She held a tray laden with small crystal glasses and sticky preserves. “Welcome. Thais is currently engaged in a study of negative space. She will join us for luncheon.”

“Negative space,” Mrs. Doukas murmured, accepting a glass. “Excellent. She has been working on her composition.”

Cornelius deposited Lukas behind a clay planter, whispering, “Stay. Do not move. Do not coo.” The pigeon blinked at him with human eyes, then settled into the dust. Cornelius returned to the Doukas parents, positioning himself at the table’s edge where he could watch both the barn and the olive tree where Pippa sometimes read.

“Thais has shown remarkable growth,” Cornelius said. He poured tea with precise, controlled movements. “Her structural analysis of the bread ovens was... geometrically sound.”

Another bray echoed from the barn. Longer this time. Desperate.

“Zeno,” Cornelius said, louder than necessary. “That is the donkey’s name. Zeno. He is... vocal. Expressing his equine truth.”

“Ah,” Mr. Doukas said. He sipped his tea. “Animal spirits. We should sketch him. The line of the neck, the sorrow in the eyes. Very moving.”

“He is not available for sketching,” Cornelius said. “He is... in character. Deeply. One must not disturb the process.”

Mrs. Doukas set down her glass. “We should settle into the Guest Lodge. We brought our own linens, of course. Hotel linens are so impersonal. We’d like to see Thais’s cabin after lunch.”

“Of course,” Imelda said.

Cornelius felt his scales flatten against his body. The Guest Lodge was the Stone Villa—the same building where Zeno was currently housed in Zeph’s room on the upper floor. The same building where Cornelius had conducted his interrogations. The same building where Maya’s empty turtle shell would be discovered if they opened the wrong door.

“The Villa is currently undergoing... humidity treatments,” Cornelius said. “For the books. Very damp weather. Unseasonable. The climate control requires... twenty-four hours to stabilize.”

“We don’t mind damp,” Mr. Doukas said. “We are ceramicists. Clay is damp.”

“Nevertheless,” Cornelius said. He stood, blocking their path to the Villa. “The Refreshment Tent offers superior ventilation. And view. Very scenic. Very... immersive.”

He guided them—herded them—toward the tent’s far side, away from the Villa, away from the barn. He spoke continuously, filling the air with words about curriculum theory, about the importance of embodied learning, about the psychological benefits of temporary zoological transmutation. He used phrases like “liminal identity states” and “therapeutic masquerade.” The Doukas parents nodded, their eyes glazing slightly, their hands moving automatically to their portfolio cases as if seeking the comfort of familiar tools.

From the barn, Zeno brayed again. Then, horribly, he spoke.

“Help,” came the voice, faint and distorted, but unmistakably human. “I’m stuck. I’m—” The words dissolved into another hee-haw.

Mr. Doukas frowned. “Did that donkey just speak?”

“Echo,” Cornelius said. His claws dug into the wooden table. “The valley. The acoustics. Very strange. Very... Cyprian. The wind carries voices from the road. That was likely a shepherd. Calling to his... flock.”

“Sounded like a boy,” Mrs. Doukas said.

“A ewe,” Cornelius said. “A young ewe. In distress. The donkey is... responding. Empathetically. As donkeys do.”

Imelda stepped forward. “Mr. Doukas, I believe Thais mentioned she wished to show you her tile studies. She has been firing pieces in the camp kiln. Very ambitious work. She will be at the Pergola shortly.”

The distraction worked. Mrs. Doukas’s face brightened. “Her glazing? She wrote that she was attempting a copper reduction.”

“Precisely,” Imelda said. “Shall we walk to the Pergola? The light is better there for viewing ceramics.”

Cornelius remained at the table, trembling. He watched them go—Imelda leading the parents toward the kitchen area, away from the Villa, away from the barn. He waited until they were out of sight, then sprinted to the Sanctuary Barn.

Zeph stood at the barn door, holding a bucket of hay, his face pale.

“Boss,” Zeph whispered. “He’s losing it. He keeps trying to talk, but it comes out wrong. And he knocked over the water trough. He’s getting stronger. More... donkey.”

Cornelius pushed past him. Inside, in the straw, Zeno stood in the center of the stall. He was small for a donkey, but his eyes—those were Zeno’s eyes, wide and terrified. The blue mane stood up in ridiculous, bright spikes.

“Zeno,” Cornelius said softly. “Can you understand me?”

Zeno nodded. He opened his mouth. “Hee-haw,” he said. Then, forcing the words: “Sorry. Was... trying... to call... for... help.”

“You must be silent,” Cornelius said. “The parents are here. Forty-eight hours early. If they discover you, the camp closes. The Showcase is void. We lose everything.”

Zeno’s ears drooped. He lowered his head.

“Keep him quiet,” Cornelius told Zeph. “No more braying. No more talking. If he must communicate, have him write. With his hoof. In the dust. But silently.”

“How do I keep a donkey from braying?” Zeph asked.

“Feed him,” Cornelius said. “Constantly. A full donkey is a quiet donkey. Use the Floaty-Loaf crumbs. Keep him docile. And move him. Take him to the upper loft. If the Doukas parents tour the Villa, they must not hear him from Zeph’s room.”

“Where else can I put him?”

Cornelius thought. The library tent? No. Too close to Maya. The pergola? No. Thais was there. The olive grove? Impossible. Pippa was there, reading her book, her vial of dust in her pocket, ready to sprinkle any crumb she found.

“The root cellar,” Cornelius said. “Beneath the Villa. Stone walls. Thick door. Move him there. Now. Before they return from the Pergola.”

Zeph stared. “The root cellar? It’s dark. And full of onions.”

“Onions will mask any... donkey aroma,” Cornelius said. “Go. Use the back stairs. I shall stall the parents.”

He rushed back outside. The Doukas parents were returning from the Pergola with Imelda, Thais walking between them, her face tense with confusion. Cornelius intercepted them at the Villa steps.

“The Guest Lodge is prepared,” he announced, his voice too loud. “We have aired the rooms. The humidity has... dissipated. Miraculously. Very dry now. Very comfortable.”

Mrs. Doukas looked at the Villa door. “We thought we heard an animal. From inside.”

“Central heating,” Cornelius said. “The furnace. It groans. Very old. Very... Cyprian.”

They ascended the stairs. Cornelius positioned himself at the rear, ready to tackle them bodily if they tried to enter Zeph’s room. But Imelda guided them left, toward the guest quarters on the eastern wing, away from the interns’ cells.

“Your room overlooks the grove,” Imelda said, opening a door. “Very peaceful. Very secure.”

Mr. Doukas entered. He set down his portfolio. He walked to the window. “Ah. One can see the barn from here. And the olive tree. Excellent composition. We shall sketch the afternoon light.”

Cornelius froze. From this window, they would see Zeph leading Zeno across the yard. They would see the donkey with the blue mane. They would see Lukas the pigeon flying loops around the pergola.

“Perhaps you would prefer the western view?” Cornelius suggested. “The sunset. Very romantic. Very... immersive.”

“We are here for the artistry,” Mrs. Doukas said, joining her husband at the window. “We wish to see the camp in its natural state. Unvarnished. Raw.”

Cornelius stepped to the window. He looked out. Zeph was emerging from the barn, leading Zeno by a rope halter. The donkey’s blue mane caught the light like a signal flare. Lukas circled overhead. In the olive grove, Pippa sat reading, her book open, her lips moving.

The camp was a minefield. And the Doukas parents had taken up residence in the observation tower.

“The natural state,” Cornelius repeated. His tail drooped. “Yes. Of course. We strive for authenticity here. Very... transformative.”

Mrs. Doukas opened her sketchbook. “We shall begin at once. Don’t mind us, dragon. We are merely observers.”

Cornelius backed toward the door. He needed to move Zeno. He needed to silence the pigeon. He needed to hide the turtle. He needed to manufacture forty-eight hours of normalcy in a camp that had become a menagerie.

He stepped into the hall and closed the door. His legs gave out. He slid down the wall until he sat upon the tiles, his wings drooping, his ledger—his third ledger of the week—digging into his side.

“Observer,” he muttered to the empty hall. “I am the observed. And I am exhausted.”

From the root cellar below, muffled by stone and onions, came a soft, despairing bray.

# Chapter 14

Chapter 14

Cornelius Drake found Cassius in the Pergola, standing motionless at the old olive press slab, his hands buried wrist-deep in dough that had long past the point of kneading. The satyr-kin’s shoulders shook. Flour dusted his beard, his horns, and the front of his apron, but the white patches were darkening where tears mixed with the fine powder, creating a paste of grief upon his cheeks.

The dough itself was wrong. Cornelius could see it from the entrance. It sagged rather than stretched, sweating a faint, anxious sheen of oil where it should have held a taut skin. The scent was not of yeast blooming in contentment, but of sharp, metallic panic—the same smell that had preceded the migrating sourdough avalanche three days prior.

Cassius did not look up. His fingers trembled within the mass. “I can feel it,” he whispered. His voice cracked like dried parchment. “The fear. It is inside me, and it is going into the dough. It will go feral. It will climb the walls again. I cannot stop it.”

Cornelius’s tail twitched once against the terracotta tiles. His first instinct was to catalog the failure, to note the viscosity of the dough and the tremor in the baker’s hands, to prescribe a solution involving temperature control and standardized hydration ratios. But the sight of Cassius—usually so warm, so solid—weeping into his craft stopped the words in Cornelius’s throat.

He stepped forward. His claws clicked against the stone. He reached up—he had to reach, for Cassius was tall even when hunched—and placed one bronze-scaled paw upon the satyr’s shoulder. The contact felt strange. Unpracticed. But necessary.

“Listen to me,” Cornelius said. He did not remove his claw. “You are not the source of the chaos. You are the anchor. The dough responds to your hands, yes, but we shall ensure those hands are steady.”

“I cannot bake if I am afraid,” Cassius said. A tear fell into the dough. “And I am afraid. The parents are here. The Showcase is tomorrow. If I fail—if the bread betrays us again—”

“Then you shall bake, and I shall lie,” Cornelius said. The declaration emerged with a certainty that surprised even him. “You focus upon the chemistry of flour and water. You focus upon the fermentation, the oven spring, the crust. You give me the bread. I shall give the parents the narrative. I shall tell them it is artisanal. I shall tell them it is deconstructed. I shall construct a framework of such elaborate pedagogical theory that they will believe the bread is meant to behave exactly as it does.”

Cassius turned his head. His eyes were red-rimmed, wet. “You would do that? You hate improvisation.”

“I hate disorder,” Cornelius corrected. “But I am learning to manage it. You bake. I shall handle the deception. We each have our craft.”

Cassius’s shoulders dropped. He exhaled, a shuddering breath that smelled of anise and yeast. His hands, still in the dough, stopped trembling. “You are a good friend, Cornelius. For a dragon who once accused my croissants of structural insufficiency.”

“They were insufficient,” Cornelius said, but his tone had softened. He removed his claw and tapped the edge of the stone slab. “Now. We require a neutralizing agent. The dough must be restarted. Discard this batch. We have time for a preferment if we begin within the hour. I shall oversee the measurements. You oversee the feeling.”

Cassius nodded. He scraped the anxious dough into the compost bin with a finality that seemed to scrape away some of his own dread. “Yes. Yes, alright. Fresh start.”

Cornelius was arranging the flour sacks in alphabetical order—rye, spelt, wheat—when Zeph arrived. The boy’s hair stood in wild peaks, and his tunic was smeared with dirt from the root cellar. He carried a hayfork over one shoulder like a soldier’s rifle, but his expression was not frightened. It was focused.

“Boss,” Zeph said. He leaned the fork against the clay oven. “We have a situation. Lukas is roosting in the Villa’s upper rafters. He keeps cooing. And Zeno… he’s digging. In the cellar. I think he’s trying to burrow through the stone wall.”

Cornelius’s scales prickled. “The Doukas parents?”

“Asleep, I think. For now. But if Lukas drops a feather on their breakfast table, or if Zeno brays again…” Zeph trailed off. He scratched his head. “We need a better cover than ‘method acting.’ They’re gonna want to see the animals up close. They’re artists. They wanna sketch ‘em.”

“What do you propose?” Cornelius asked. He folded his wings tight against his body, waiting.

Zeph straightened. He looked at Cassius, who was measuring water with careful, steady hands, then back to Cornelius. “A petting zoo,” he said. “We lean into it. We say it’s part of the curriculum. A menagerie for sensory education. The kids are learning animal husbandry through… immersive proximity. Or something.”

Cornelius tilted his head. “You wish to present the transformed campers as intentional exhibits?”

“We gotta hide ’em in plain sight,” Zeph said. His voice gained confidence. “Lukas is just a really friendly pigeon with a bandana. Zeno’s a therapy donkey. Maya’s a… a tactile sculpture for the visually impaired. We set up stations. We control the narrative. You’re the Ringmaster, Boss. You direct the parents to look where we want. And I… I’ll manage the animals. Keep ’em calm. Keep ’em fed. Keep ’em quiet.”

Cornelius studied the boy. Zeph’s hands were steady. His eyes were bloodshot, but clear. He was not the fumbling intern who had dropped the Floaty-Loaf three days ago. He was not the child who had fallen asleep on stakeout duty. He was something else. A guardian, perhaps. A keeper.

“You would be the Ringmaster,” Cornelius said quietly. “Not I.”

“Someone’s gotta handle the paperwork and the parents,” Zeph said. “You’re better at the lying. I’m better at the… the creature stuff. It’s in my blood, maybe. The lamassu thing. I can calm Zeno. I can keep Lukas from flying into the Doukas’ window. But I gotta be official. I need you to say I’m in charge of the menagerie. Give me the title. Makes it easier to boss the campers around if they think I’m not just the intern.”

Cornelius reached into his waistcoat. He withdrew his pen—a heavy, brass instrument—and held it out. “You are hereby appointed Ringmaster of the Camp Olea Educational Menagerie. You have authority over all fauna, domesticated or transformed. You report only to me. You are responsible for their safety, their silence, and their presentation. Do you accept?”

Zeph took the pen. He turned it over in his fingers, then tucked it behind his ear. “Yeah, Boss. I accept.” He almost smiled. “I’ll go check on the donkey. Make sure he doesn’t kick through the cellar wall.”

“Zeph,” Cornelius said as the boy turned to leave.

“Yeah?”

“You are doing well,” Cornelius said. The words felt formal, but he meant them. “It is commendable.”

Zeph’s ears reddened. He nodded, once, and disappeared into the dark toward the Villa.

The night settled over Camp Olea with the weight of a closed book. The Doukas parents had retired to their room in the eastern wing, their sketchbooks filled with ominous studies of olive branches and shadow. Cassius worked in the Pergola by lamplight, his hands steady now, the new dough rising in proofing baskets with placid, yeasty patience. Zeph was in the root cellar, speaking softly to Zeno, the sound of his voice a low hum against the stone.

Cornelius sat upon the Villa’s porch steps. His third ledger of the week—the one he had started after the ink spill—dug into his side, a hard rectangle of pressure against his ribs. He did not open it. He did not catalog. He simply sat, his wings drooping against the cool terracotta, his spectacles sliding down his snout. He was too tired to push them up.

The air smelled of dust and old paper. Exhaustion settled into his bones, a heaviness that felt like it might fossilize him there, turning him into a statue of a dragon upon the steps, a guardian who had failed to guard against the one thing he had not catalogued: a child’s wish for a happy ending.

A small sound disturbed the silence. The scuff of a shoe. The rustle of pages.

Pippa stood at the bottom of the steps. She wore her nightdress, and her pigtails were loose, hair framing her face like a halo of dark silk. She clutched the old, gold-edged storybook to her chest. The book seemed heavier than she was, a repository of all the narrative weight that had unmade the camp.

“Mr. Cornelius?” she whispered.

Cornelius looked at her. He did not have the energy for interrogation. He did not have the energy for suspicion. He simply looked.

“I cannot sleep,” she said. “The stories are too loud. They want to be told. And I thought… you look sad. And tired. And I thought maybe you would like a story. Instead of more… inventory.”

Cornelius stared at her. He should refuse. He should order her to bed. He should confiscate the book. He should do a hundred things that were logical and protective and controlling.

Instead, he sighed. It was a small sound, barely a breath. “Very well,” he said. “One story. But no sprinkles. No dust. Just words.”

Pippa’s eyes widened. She climbed the steps, careful not to touch him, and sat two tiles away. She placed the book between them. The pages were soft with wear, the illustrations catching the lamplight from the window: swans, bakers, ovens, feathers.

“It is called The Baker’s Dozen Swans,” Pippa said. She opened the book. The paper smelled of vanilla and burnt sugar. “It is about a baker who loves his swans too much. And how he loses them. And how he gets them back.”

Cornelius adjusted his spectacles. He rested his claws upon his knees. He was too exhausted to maintain his vigilance, too broken to insist upon order. He nodded. “Proceed,” he said. “Read.”

Pippa took a breath. She began to speak, her voice soft and steady in the darkness, weaving a tale of feathers and flour, of spells that bound and words that freed. Cornelius listened, his head drooping, his scales settling into the cool stone. He did not notice when his tail stopped twitching. He did not notice when his eyes began to close. He only listened, letting the story wash over him like a tide, carrying him toward whatever morning would bring.

# Chapter 15

Chapter 15

Cornelius Drake sat upon the terracotta tiles of the Stone Villa porch, his wings folded tight against his sides, his spectacles sliding down his snout. He lacked the energy to push them up. The night air pressed heavy against his scales, thick with the scent of olive leaves and the distant, yeasty promise of Cassius’s emergency dough. Inside the Villa, the Doukas parents slept in the eastern wing, their sketchbooks closed but their artistic scrutiny merely paused. Below them, in the root cellar, Zeno the donkey shifted against stone walls, his hooves scraping a nervous rhythm against the dark.

Cornelius should have been cataloging. He should have been updating his third ledger with the day’s disasters, noting the viscosity of fear in Cassius’s aborted dough, or the precise decibel level of Zeno’s bray. Instead, he sat frozen, a bronze statue of exhaustion, while the sounds of the camp settled around him like dust upon a neglected manuscript.

Pippa sat two tiles away. She had not moved since beginning her story, her small body curled around the gold-edged book like a protective sigil. The lamplight from the window behind them caught the gilt lettering on the spine: *The Baker’s Dozen Swans*. The pages were soft with wear, their edges feathered from countless thumbings.

“Once,” Pippa whispered, “there was a Prince who loved the sky.”

Cornelius’s ear twitched. He did not open his eyes. He was too tired to maintain his vigilance, too depleted to insist upon order. He let the words wash over him, a tide of narrative carrying him toward unconsciousness.

“He climbed the highest towers,” Pippa read. “He measured the clouds. He wished only to fly above his father’s kingdom, to see the world from above, to be as light as thistledown. And so, on the night of the great feast, when the Baker served the sweetbread, the Prince ate a crumb that had been blessed by the wind. He turned into a bird with a blue scarf about his neck, and he flew from the highest window, never to land again in the same place twice.”

Cornelius’s eyes opened. His tail, which had been still against the tiles, gave a single, sharp twitch.

He sat up. The movement was jerky, uncoordinated, sending his spectacles askew. He pushed them back into place with one claw, staring at Pippa. The girl did not look up. Her finger traced the woodcut illustration on the page: a young man in princely raiment, half-transformed into a pigeon, a scrap of blue cloth tied about his throat.

“Lukas,” Cornelius said. His voice emerged as a rasp, stripped of its usual formal precision.

Pippa nodded, still reading. “The Prince was happy, but he was lost. He could not remember how to be a boy. He only knew the wind.”

Cornelius’s scales prickled upright along his spine. He leaned forward, his exhaustion evaporating like morning dew from hot stone. The scent reached him then—that distinct, sharp aroma he had first detected in Chapter Two, clinging to the rogue crumb from Lukas’s pillow. *Sea salt and old paper*. The smell of archives, of scrolls, of stories leaking into reality.

“Continue,” Cornelius commanded. He did not mean to bark, but the word came out sharp, a librarian’s order to a recalcitrant patron.

Pippa turned the page. The paper made a sound like a sigh. “There was also a Knight,” she read, her voice dropping to a reverent hush. “A strong Knight who carried the burdens of all the realm. He bore the sacks of flour, the barrels of wine, the weight of every sorrow. He never complained. But he was tired. So tired. And on the night of the feast, the Baker gave him a muffin with a burden baked inside. The Knight became a small donkey with a blue mane, strong and stubborn, carrying loads forever, unable to speak his own name.”

Zeno. The donkey in the cellar. The boy who had eaten the Mood-Muffins decorated with iridescent dust.

Cornelius felt his claws dig into the terracotta. A chip of tile cracked beneath his grip. “The Lady,” he said. “Is there a Lady in this tale?”

Pippa looked up, her eyes wide and dark in the lamplight. “Yes,” she said. “The Lady who loved sleep. Who was afraid of the dark, and so hid inside her shell. She ate the sleeping cake and became a turtle, carrying her home upon her back, retreating into silence.”

Maya. The turtle in the ventilated box in the library tent.

The pattern clicked into place with the force of a dwarven lock engaging. Cornelius stood up. His legs trembled, but he stood, his wings half-furled for balance. He looked down at the book, at Pippa’s small hands holding it, at the woodcut illustrations that seemed to shift in the flickering light.

“It is not the sprinkles,” Cornelius said. His voice was hollow with realization. “It was never the sprinkles. It was the *text*.”

He had been so certain. He had cataloged the violet-green dust, the iridescent vials, the decorative sugar. He had blamed the medium, the carrier, the vector. He had tested the muffins, analyzed the crumbs, confiscated the glass vials. But he had allowed the book to remain. He had said, “Just words, no sprinkles,” believing that without the dust, the danger was contained.

He had not accounted for a child’s wish for a happy ending. He had not accounted for Narrative Physics.

“You are not a saboteur,” Cornelius said, looking at Pippa with new eyes. “You are a... a Narrative Mage. An unconscious channel. The stories are not tales you read. They are instructions you give to reality.”

Pippa blinked. “I just read the stories,” she said. “I just want them to have happy endings. The Prince should fly. The Knight should be strong. The Lady should be safe.”

“But they are not safe,” Cornelius said. He stepped closer, his shadow falling across the open pages. “They are transformed. They are lost inside their roles. Just as Lukas is lost in feathers, and Zeno is lost in braying, and Maya is lost in her shell.”

He looked at the book. The scent of burnt sugar and ancient paper grew stronger, heady and cloying. It was the smell of magic activating, of narrative asserting itself over the physical world.

“What happens next?” Cornelius demanded. He reached out with one claw, hovering over the page but not touching it, as if the paper might burn him. “What happens at the end of this tale? After the feast? After the transformations?”

Pippa turned the page. Her finger traced the final illustration. “The Baker’s Dozen Swans,” she read, her voice trembling now. “The Baker loved his swans too much. He baked them into a great cake, a cake so light it floated. And when the King ate of it, the entire kingdom—the castle, the towers, the gardens, all the people—rose into the sky and flew to the moon, where they would be safe forever from sorrow and harm.”

Cornelius went very still. His tail froze mid-twitch. The air in his throat felt like sand.

“The moon,” he repeated.

“To be safe,” Pippa whispered. “So nothing bad can ever happen. A happy ending.”

Cornelius looked toward the kitchen. Through the window, he could see Cassius moving in the lamplight, preparing the dough for tomorrow’s Parent Showcase. The final cake. The great feast. The culminating moment when all the parents—the Doukas parents, the others who would arrive—would gather to eat the children’s creations.

If Pippa read that ending, if she reached the final page while the cake was being served, while the feast was underway...

The camp would fly away. The Stone Villa, the Sanctuary Barn, the library tent with all his books, the children, the parents, the entire compound—it would all lift into the air like thistledown and vanish into the lunar void. All to satisfy a child’s wish for safety. All to give them a “happy ending” free from harm.

Chapter 16

It tasted of salt. Sharp. Crystalline. Bleakly mineral.

“Once,” he whispered, selecting a fresh pinch, “there was a grain of sugar. It was sweet. It dissolved upon the tongue like a snowflake in summer, leaving only the memory of sweetness and the chemical formula C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁. It was harmless. It was sucrose.”

Thirteen servings. Thirteen swans. The number hummed with narrative significance.

With the frosting, the decorative elements, the internal structure, and the inevitable accretion of magical resonance that would occur during the Parent Showcase feast, the mass would be sufficient to serve as a levitation device for the entire camp compound.

“The kingdom rises,” Cornelius whispered, recalling Pippa’s words. “The castle, the towers, the gardens, all the people—rose into the sky and flew to the moon.”

If she finished the story while this cake was being consumed—if she read the final page as the parents lifted their forks, as the Doukas parents tasted Cassius’s masterpiece—the narrative would complete itself. The cake would become the vehicle. The camp would become the kingdom. And they would all fly away. Not metaphorically. Not poetically. Physically. Two hundred feet of stone and timber, of children and parents and books, lifting into the thermosphere on a pillar of narrative thrust, bound for the airless silence of the lunar surface.

To be safe. To be shelved.

Cornelius’s claws dug into the press slab, seeking purchase against a vertigo that had nothing to do with altitude and everything to do with responsibility. He had been wrong. He had spent a week hunting for a saboteur, for a culprit with malice aforethought. He had accused Zeno of chromatic terrorism. He had suspected Kyria of Council espionage. He had cataloged the sprinkles as poison when they were merely paper—blank, waiting, desperate for a story to tell.

And now the story was written. The cake was baked. The ending approached.

He looked at the sleeping Cassius. The baker had trusted him. “You bake, I shall lie,” Cornelius had promised. But lies would not stop a Bardic narrative. Only a better story could do that. A counter-narrative. An edit in the margins, as he had told Pippa.

Pippa was nine years old. She possessed an ancient book with gold-edged pages. She possessed a vial of reagent-grade narrative dust. She possessed, apparently, the innate ability to impress reality with her wishes. Such power did not emerge from nothing. It was inherited. It was trained, or it was bound.

Cornelius removed his spectacles and cleaned them with his handkerchief. His hands trembled, just slightly, a tremor he had not experienced since his first day as a Library Dragon, when he had been tasked with cataloging the Lost Epics of Thrace and had realized the weight of preserving what could not be replaced.

He needed lineage. He needed genealogy. He needed to know whose bloodline had produced a child who could rewrite physics with a fairy tale and a sugar cookie.

Behind him, the cake gleamed in the starlight, a monument to potential energy, waiting for the word that would launch it.

Cornelius disappeared into the dark, his mind already sorting indices, already searching for a story strong enough to hold the ground beneath their feet.

And unless we teach her to edit her own story within the next three hours, we are all going to fly to the moon and die.”

# Chapter 19

Chapter 19

Cornelius Drake stood upon the terracotta tiles of the Pergola and watched the red light fade from Pippa’s pockets. The arterial glow dimmed to a soft, pulsing pink—the color of dawn viewed through closed eyelids. Mrs. Miller’s arms remained wrapped around her daughter, her linen dress pooling on the stone like a spill of unbleached parchment. The humming continued, low and resonant, a sound that seemed to vibrate in the mortar between the stones.

The book lay on the floor, its gold-edged pages darkened where Pippa’s tears had fallen. The scent of ozone and burning paper—that sharp, dangerous aroma of narrative run wild—dissipated, replaced by the mundane, grounding smell of spilled coffee and yeast.

“Three hours,” Cornelius said. His voice emerged steadier than he felt. “Correction. We now possess approximately sixty minutes until the Bake-Off judging commences. The parents are assembling. Master Orestes has likely arrived with his truth-sensing sinuses.”

Mrs. Miller looked up from Pippa’s shoulder. Her eyes were fierce, terrified, and perfectly focused. “Tell me how to fix my children.”

Cornelius adjusted his spectacles. His tail twitched once against a sack of flour, sending up a small puff of white dust. “We require a counter-narrative. The Final Feast cake is keyed to a story of flight and escape. We must bake an alternative ending.”

He turned to the three-tier monstrosity dominating the olive press slab. The sugar swans trembled slightly, their wings still angled for departure. The cake hummed with potential energy, a reservoir of forty-seven pounds of flour and butter waiting to enact Pippa’s original story.

“We cannot simply destroy it,” Cornelius continued, gesturing with one claw. “The narrative thrust is too powerful. It must be redirected. Consumed. Rewritten from within.”

Cassius emerged from behind the fermentation vats, his hooves clicking against the tiles. His hands were still dusted with flour, his horns wrapped in gauze. “A Reversal Cake,” he whispered. His voice cracked. “I have heard of such things. In the old texts. A cake that un-eats a transformation. But it requires a foundation of absolute grounding. Earth-magic. Not the airy levitation of the swans.”

Mrs. Miller stood, lifting Pippa with her. The child clung to her mother’s waist, her face pressed against the linen. “My mother,” Mrs. Miller said, her voice gaining strength. “She did not sing wheat into flour to make it fly. She sang to make it *ground*. To make it nourish. Root-vegetable magic. Carrots. Beets.”

Cornelius felt his scales prickle upright. “Carrot cake,” he said. “Dense. Earth-bound. Rich in beta-carotene and gravitational certainty.”

“Exactly,” Mrs. Miller said. She looked down at Pippa. “Baby, do you have your vials? The sparkly dust?”

Pippa nodded, her hand moving to her pinafore pocket. She withdrew the glass vial. The dust inside was no longer violet-green or arterial red. It had shifted to a soft, iridescent pink—the color of comfort, of skin-warmed blankets, of a mug of tea held in steady hands.

“It’s listening,” Pippa whispered. “The story is listening to Mommy.”

“We shall teach it a new ending,” Cornelius said. He turned to Cassius. “Can you construct a carrot cake of sufficient density to anchor three transformed children and one floating boy?”

Cassius wiped his forehead, leaving a streak of flour. “If... if I have help. If the dough is not anxious. If—”

“You will have help,” Cornelius said. He looked at the assembled group. “We require a division of labor. A heist, if you will, executed with bibliographic precision.”

Imelda appeared in the doorway of the Pergola, her presence as sudden and calming as a bookmark sliding into place. She carried an armful of aprons. “The judges are arriving. The Doukas parents are setting up their sketching easels near the Villa. Master Orestes is questioning Kyria about the health code violations. We have perhaps fifty-five minutes.”

“Imelda,” Cornelius said. “You shall manage the perimeter. Delay the judging. Cite... humidity concerns. Dust particles. Any bureaucratic irregularity necessary.”

Imelda nodded, her silver hair catching the kitchen light. “I shall deploy the Index of Minor Infractions.”

“Cassius,” Cornelius continued. “You shall prepare the batter. Mrs. Miller, you shall provide the harmonic resonance—the stirring, the singing, the maternal force required to ground the magic.”

“What about me?” Pippa asked. Her voice was small but no longer broken.

Cornelius reached into his waistcoat and withdrew the brass pen. He held it out, the metal gleaming with a soft, steady light. “You shall edit the manuscript, Pippa. Not with tears this time. With intent. You shall inscribe the new ending directly into the batter using your dust and this pen. You shall write that the Prince returns from the sky. That the Knight lays down his burdens and speaks his name. That the Lady wakes and walks upon the earth.”

Pippa took the pen. Her fingers closed around it with sudden determination. “And the Baker’s Dozen Swans?”

“The swans remain,” Cornelius said. “But they do not fly to the moon. They swim in a lake. A very small, very grounded lake. Made of cream cheese frosting.”

Zeph burst into the Pergola, his hair standing on end, his shirt covered in straw. “The animals are restless,” he panted. “Zeno is digging through the root cellar floor. Lukas keeps trying to fly into the Villa’s upper windows. And Maya... I think Maya is trying to hibernate inside an olive jar.”

Zeph straightened his spine. He had been carrying the brass pen only yesterday, but now he carried authority differently. “I can get them here. But Cornelius... they’re scared. Zeno keeps braying. He sounds... he sounds like he’s forgetting.”

“Then we must remind him,” Cornelius said. “With sugar and story. Move.”

The kitchen erupted into coordinated chaos.

Cassius flew to the storage bins, his hooves skidding on the tiles. He dragged out sacks of carrots, walnuts, and spices. Mrs. Miller rolled up her sleeves and seized a wooden spoon the size of a cricket bat. She began to hum—not the tuneless sound of before, but a specific, rhythmic melody, the song her mother had used to grind wheat. The sound seemed to settle the flour in its bins, to weight the sugar crystals with extra gravity.

Cornelius positioned himself before the clay beehive ovens. He extended his claws and began to regulate the temperature with precise, draconic control. He blew a thin stream of blue flame—not the feeble puff he had produced during orientation, but a steady, controlled jet of heat that warmed the stone without scorching it. The ovens glowed with a steady, domestic orange.

“Temperature at three hundred and seventy-five degrees,” Cornelius announced. “Regulated to the half-degree. Proceed with mixing.”

Cassius grated carrots with desperate speed, the orange shreds piling up like autumn leaves. Mrs. Miller cracked eggs into a stone bowl, her movements sharp and definitive. Each crack echoed like a punctuation mark. Pippa stood on a stool beside her, the brass pen in one hand and the vial of pink dust in the other.

“What do I write?” Pippa asked.

“The truth,” Cornelius said. He monitored the oven flames, his eyes reflecting the orange glow. “Write that Lukas likes cookies, but he likes them on the ground. Write that Zeno is strong enough to carry his own name. Write that Maya is safe because she is awake, not because she is hidden.”

Pippa uncorked the vial. She sprinkled the pink dust into the batter. It did not glow red or violet. It shimmered like mica in granite—solid, earthy, real. She dipped the brass pen into the mixture and began to stir, her wrist moving in small, precise circles. As she stirred, she whispered.

“Once, there was a boy who was a bird,” she said. “But the ground was soft. The ground was kind. The boy remembered that he had feet. And he came down to walk.”

The batter thickened. The scent of cinnamon and nutmeg filled the Pergola, overwhelming the lingering traces of sea salt and ancient paper. The smell was dense, moist, rooted. It smelled of cellars and harvests and mothers.

“Faster,” Imelda called from the doorway. “Master Orestes is sneezing. He suspects something is amiss with the soufflé presentation.”

Cassius poured the batter into three round pans. Cornelius slid them into the ovens with surgical precision, his claws clicking against the metal racks. “Baking time: twenty-five minutes. No more, no less. Zeph, status of the menagerie?”

He was followed by Lukas the pigeon, who hopped along the ground rather than flying, his blue bandana dragging in the dust. Behind them, Zeph carried a terracotta pot in which Maya the turtle had retreated, her shell barely visible.

“They’re here,” Zeph said. “But Cornelius... look at Zeno.”

The small donkey was changing. The blue of his mane was fading, becoming patchy. His hooves were scraping the tiles with a sound that was less equine and more... desperate. He was losing the shape of the Knight, forgetting the story Pippa had written for him.

“He is reverting to the original narrative,” Cornelius said. He checked the oven timer. “The cake must hurry.”

The minutes ticked by with agonizing slowness. Mrs. Miller sang louder. Cassius prepared the cream cheese frosting, beating it with a whisk until his arms trembled. Pippa stood before the oven, the brass pen clutched in both hands, her lips moving silently as she rehearsed the new ending.

Cornelius monitored the heat. His scales felt tight against his body. The pressure of the impending deadline sat upon his chest like a heavy book. He could hear the murmur of voices from the Villa—the parents gathering, the judges assembling their clipboards.

“Time,” Cornelius announced.

He opened the ovens. The carrot cakes emerged, golden-brown and dense, radiating warmth that smelled of earth and safety. Cassius slathered them with frosting, his hands moving in frantic, swirling motions. Mrs. Miller produced a handful of walnuts and pressed them into the sides, each nut clicking into place like a rivet.

Pippa stepped forward. She opened her vial and sprinkled the last of the pink dust over the top tier. Then, with the brass pen, she wrote in the frosting. Not words, exactly, but symbols—small, precise edits. Crossings-out. A swan with its wings folded. A boy with feet on the ground.

“It’s done,” she whispered.

“Zeph,” Cornelius commanded. “Administer the cure.”

Zeph cut the cake into rough slices. He placed the first piece before Zeno the donkey. The animal sniffed it, his ears flattening. Then, with a desperate motion, he bit into the cake.

For a moment, nothing happened. Then the blue mane flickered. The donkey’s form seemed to fold inward, like a page being turned. There was a sound like a book snapping shut.

Zeno stood there. Human. Fourteen years old. Covered in flour and straw and donkey hair, but unmistakably human. His hair was still dyed bright blue, but it was hair, not fur. He looked at his hands. He opened his mouth.

“I,” he said. His voice cracked. “I am Zeno. I am... I am me.”

“Next,” Cornelius said, his heart hammering against his ribs. “Quickly.”

Zeph placed a slice before Lukas the pigeon. The bird pecked at the frosting, then tore at the cake with sudden ferocity. The blue bandana slipped from his neck. There was a puff of pink dust, a smell of cinnamon, and then Lukas was sitting on the tiles, fourteen years old, gangly and human, wearing a tunic that was far too large and covered in feathers.

“Pippa?” he said, looking at his sister.

Pippa ran to him. She threw her arms around his neck. “I’m sorry,” she sobbed. “I’m sorry, I’m sorry.”

Lukas hugged her back, his eyes wide with confusion and relief. “You gave me cookies,” he said. “They were good cookies. But I like them better when I have hands.”

“Maya,” Cornelius urged. “The turtle.”

Zeph placed a small piece of cake on a saucer and slid it into the terracotta pot. For a long moment, the turtle remained withdrawn. Then a small head emerged. She took a bite.

The shell did not fade; it simply... became a dress. A green dress with a pattern that looked like scutes. Maya sat up, twelve years old, blinking in the light, her hair tangled with bits of lettuce.

“I was asleep,” she said. “But I’m awake now. I think... I think I want to be awake.”

“Bennie,” Zeph said, offering the last slice to the small boy who had been bouncing for days.

Cornelius felt his knees weaken. He gripped the edge of the olive press slab to steady himself. The Reversal Cake worked. The children were restored. The narrative had been edited successfully.

But they were not safe yet.

The children sat upon the tiles, disheveled, half-naked in some cases, covered in animal hair and frosting and confusion. Zeno wore only a torn tunic that had fit a donkey. Lukas was swimming in his feather-covered clothes. Maya’s green dress was stained with olive brine.

Imelda appeared in the doorway. Her eyes swept over the scene—the restored children, the triumphant adults, the half-eaten cake—and she smiled. It was a sharp, efficient smile.

“Master Orestes has concluded his inspection of the soufflés,” she announced. “He is turning toward the Pergola. He will be here in ninety seconds.”

Cornelius felt his blood run cold. “They cannot be seen like this. They are naked. They are feral. They are covered in narrative residue.”

“Aprons,” Imelda said.

She threw them.

The garments sailed through the air in a perfect arc—a blue one for Zeno, a red one for Lukas, a green one for Maya, and a small yellow one for Bennie. They landed on the children’s heads, covering their confusion, their nakedness, their recent status as beasts of burden and flight.

“Put them on,” Imelda commanded. “Stand up. Wipe your faces. You are not campers who were recently animals. You are apprentices. You have been... taste-testing. Vigorously.”

Zeno pulled the blue apron over his head. It hung to his knees. He looked at Cornelius, his eyes still holding the wildness of the donkey, but also a new, human understanding. “I remember,” he said. “I remember everything. The hay. The dark. Thank you.”

“Later,” Cornelius said. His voice was tight. “You may thank me later. For now, assume the posture of culinary students. Pippa, hide that pen. Mrs. Miller, resume humming. Cassius, look exhausted but triumphant. Zeph, stand behind them like a supervisor.”

He turned to the olive press slab. The Final Feast cake still stood there, the sugar swans looking suddenly less like creatures of flight and more like decorative, edible statues. The Reversal Cake sat beside it, half-consumed, steaming slightly in the morning air.

Cornelius adjusted his spectacles. He smoothed his waistcoat. He checked his claws for frosting residue and found them clean.

Master Orestes rounded the corner of the Pergola, his Sphinx-kin features severe, his nose twitching.

Cornelius stepped forward, placing himself between the judges and the children. He executed a small, precise bow.

“Master Orestes,” he said. “Welcome to the Bake-Off. I trust you find the humidity within acceptable parameters?”

Orestes looked at the children in their aprons, at the carrot cake, at the sugar swans. He sniffed the air. Cornelius held his breath.

The Sphinx sneezed.

But it was a small sneeze. A dust sneeze. Not a lie sneeze.

“Acceptable,” Orestes said. “Proceed with the judging.”

Cornelius exhaled. Behind him, he heard Pippa whisper to her mother, “Is it over?”

Mrs. Miller’s hand found her daughter’s shoulder. “No, baby,” she said softly. “It’s just beginning. But you’re grounded now. We’re all grounded.”

Cornelius allowed himself a small, private smile. Then he turned to face the judges, his claws clicking against the terracotta tiles in a rhythm of triumph.

# Chapter 20

Chapter 20

The Olive Grove presented a tableau of calculated chaos. Cornelius Drake adjusted his spectacles, ensuring the lenses sat precisely upon his snout, and surveyed the scene. The ancient trees stood in their rows, their leaves creating a canopy of shifting shadows that dappled the terracotta tiles below. The air hung heavy with the scent of rosemary and baked dough—humid, yes, still humid, but Cornelius found he could no longer muster the energy to catalog the specific percentage of moisture in the atmosphere. Master Orestes sat at the center of the long wooden bench, his Sphinx-kin features impassive, his paws resting upon a leather portfolio. To his left sat the Doukas parents, their sketchbooks open, their eyes bright with the particular hunger of artists who have stumbled upon an unexpected spectacle. To Orestes’s right perched Little Kyria, her notebook at the ready, her green complexion somewhat paler than usual—whether from the morning’s exertions or from having witnessed the carrot cake incident, Cornelius could not determine.

“These,” Zeno announced, with a gestures that Cornelius recognized immediately as his own—broad, sweeping, and technically accurate if one squinted—“are Sourdough Sculptures. Rustic charm. Master Orestes leaned forward, his nose twitching. He did not sneeze. “They are lumpy,” he observed.

“They are *authentic*,” Zeno corrected, without a trace of fear. “Perfection is a myth propagated by industrial bakeries. Real bread has character. These stones have stories.”

Little Kyria made a note. Her pencil scratched against the paper. Cornelius found himself holding his breath, his claws digging into the soft earth beneath the olive tree where he stood.

“Proceed,” Orestes said finally.

Zeph had become the Ringmaster in truth, not merely in title.

Imelda had revived and was already reorganizing the library tent, her voice carrying softly as she cataloged the day’s events. Pippa’s laughter echoed from the grove, high and clear and entirely human.

The camp was messy. It was humid. It was alive.

Cornelius Drake, Library Dragon, guardian of the hoard, lit his pipe again and watched the shadows deepen. The books were safe. The children were safe. And for now, that was enough.
